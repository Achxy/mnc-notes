## 1. The problem CRT solves

We are given several congruences for an unknown integer $x$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x &\equiv a_1 \pmod{m_1},\\
x &\equiv a_2 \pmod{m_2},\\
&\vdots \\
x &\equiv a_r \pmod{m_r}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Question:

1. When does a **simultaneous solution** $x$ exist?
2. If it exists, how many such $x$ are there?

The Chinese Remainder Theorem gives a complete answer when the moduli are pairwise coprime.

We assume throughout this main statement that

$$
\gcd(m_i, m_j) = 1 \quad \text{for all } i \ne j.
$$

---

## 2. Statement of the Chinese Remainder Theorem

Let $m_1, \dots, m_r$ be pairwise coprime positive integers and let $a_1, \dots, a_r$ be any integers.

Define

$$
M = m_1 m_2 \cdots m_r.
$$

**Theorem (CRT).**

1. There exists an integer $x$ satisfying all congruences
   $$
   x \equiv a_i \pmod{m_i} \quad (i=1,\dots,r).
   $$
2. Any two such solutions are **congruent modulo $M$**:
   $$
   x_1, x_2 \text{ both solutions} \;\Rightarrow\; x_1 \equiv x_2 \pmod{M}.
   $$
   So there is a **unique solution modulo $M$**.

We now prove this carefully and construct $x$ explicitly.

---

## 3. Construction of a solution (existence)

We want to build an $x$ that hits each $a_i$ modulo $m_i$.

### Step 1: Define the partial moduli

For each $i$, set

$$
M_i = \frac{M}{m_i}
= m_1 \cdots m_{i-1} m_{i+1} \cdots m_r.
$$

So $M_i$ is the product of all moduli **except** $m_i$.

Because the $m_j$ are pairwise coprime:

- Every prime factor of $m_i$ is missing from $M_i$,
- So
  $$
  \gcd(M_i, m_i) = 1.
  $$

### Step 2: Inverses modulo each $m_i$

Since $\gcd(M_i, m_i)=1$, by the existence of modular inverses:

There is an integer $N_i$ such that

$$
M_i N_i \equiv 1 \pmod{m_i}.
$$

(We can find each $N_i$ using the extended Euclidean algorithm.)

### Step 3: Candidate solution

Define

$$
x = \sum_{i=1}^r a_i M_i N_i.
$$

Check what $x$ does modulo each $m_j$:

For fixed $j$,

- If $i \ne j$, then $M_i$ contains factor $m_j$, so
  $$
  M_i \equiv 0 \pmod{m_j}.
  $$
- Therefore all terms with $i\ne j$ vanish modulo $m_j$.

So

$$
x \equiv a_j M_j N_j \pmod{m_j}.
$$

But by construction

$$
M_j N_j \equiv 1 \pmod{m_j},
$$

hence

$$
x \equiv a_j \pmod{m_j}.
$$

This holds for every $j$, so $x$ is a simultaneous solution.

Existence is proven, with an explicit formula.

---

## 4. Uniqueness modulo $M$

Let $x_1$ and $x_2$ both satisfy

$$
x_k \equiv a_i \pmod{m_i} \quad \text{for all } i.
$$

Then for each $i$,

$$
x_1 \equiv a_i \equiv x_2 \pmod{m_i}
\;\Rightarrow\;
m_i \mid (x_1 - x_2).
$$

So every $m_i$ divides $(x_1-x_2)$.

Because the $m_i$ are pairwise coprime, their product $M$ also divides $(x_1 - x_2)$:

$$
M \mid (x_1 - x_2).
$$

Thus

$$
x_1 \equiv x_2 \pmod{M}.
$$

So all solutions are the same modulo $M$; there is a unique residue class modulo $M$ containing all solutions.

Uniqueness is proven.

## 5. Example 1 — Classic small system

Solve
$$
x \equiv 1 \pmod{3},\qquad
x \equiv 2 \pmod{5},\qquad
x \equiv 3 \pmod{7}.
$$

Here $m_1=3$, $m_2=5$, $m_3=7$ are pairwise coprime.  
$M = 3\cdot5\cdot7 = 105$.

Compute:

- $M_1 = 105/3 = 35$.
  Need $N_1$ with $35 N_1 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$.
  Since $35 \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$, we need $2N_1\equiv1\pmod{3}$, so $N_1\equiv2\pmod3$.
  Take $N_1=2$.

- $M_2 = 105/5 = 21$.
  $21 \equiv 1 \pmod5$, so $N_2\equiv1\pmod5$.
  Take $N_2=1$.

- $M_3 = 105/7 = 15$.
  $15 \equiv 1 \pmod7$, so $N_3\equiv1\pmod7$.
  Take $N_3=1$.

Now

$$
x = a_1 M_1 N_1 + a_2 M_2 N_2 + a_3 M_3 N_3
= 1\cdot35\cdot2 + 2\cdot21\cdot1 + 3\cdot15\cdot1.
$$

Compute:

$$
x = 70 + 42 + 45 = 157.
$$

Reduce modulo $105$:

$$
157 \equiv 157 - 105 = 52 \pmod{105}.
$$

So the solution set is

$$
x \equiv 52 \pmod{105}.
$$

You can quickly check:
$52 \equiv 1 \pmod3$, $52 \equiv 2 \pmod5$, $52 \equiv 3 \pmod7$.

## 6. Example 2 — Using CRT with linear congruences

Example from your notes:

Solve the system
$$
2x \equiv 1 \pmod{5},\quad
3x \equiv 9 \pmod{6},\quad
4x \equiv 1 \pmod{7},\quad
5x \equiv 9 \pmod{11}.
$$

Step 1: simplify each congruence individually.

1. $2x \equiv 1 \pmod{5}$: since $\gcd(2,5)=1$, multiply by $2^{-1}\equiv3$:
   $$
   x \equiv 3 \pmod{5}.
   $$

2. $3x \equiv 9 \pmod{6}$: here $\gcd(3,6)=3$ and $3\mid 9$, divide by $3$:
   $$
   x \equiv 3 \pmod{2}.
   $$

3. $4x \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$: $\gcd(4,7)=1$, inverse of $4$ mod $7$ is $2$ (since $4\cdot2=8\equiv1$), so
   $$
   x \equiv 2 \pmod{7}.
   $$

4. $5x \equiv 9 \pmod{11}$: inverse of $5$ mod $11$ is $9$ (since $5\cdot9=45\equiv1$), so
   $$
   x \equiv 9\cdot9 \equiv 81 \equiv 4 \pmod{11}.
   $$

So we have reduced to

$$
x \equiv 3 \pmod{5},\quad
x \equiv 3 \pmod{2},\quad
x \equiv 2 \pmod{7},\quad
x \equiv 4 \pmod{11}.
$$

Note:

- $x \equiv 3 \pmod{2}$ means $x$ is odd, no need to keep it separately once included in the combined modulus.
- The moduli $5,2,7,11$ are pairwise coprime.

Set $M = 5\cdot2\cdot7\cdot11 = 770$.

You can now compute $M_i, N_i$ and form

$$
x = \sum a_i M_i N_i \pmod{770},
$$

obtaining (as in your notes) the solution

$$
x \equiv 653 \pmod{770}.
$$

(Any intermediate arithmetic you had matches this construction.)

## 7. Word-problem example — Eggs in a basket

Problem sketch (from your notes, formalized):

Find the smallest positive integer $x$ such that

- $x \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$,
- $x \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$,
- $x \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$,
- $x \equiv 4 \pmod{5}$,
- $x \equiv 5 \pmod{6}$,
- $x \equiv 0 \pmod{7}$.

Observations:

1. The first five conditions say:
   $$
   x+1 \equiv 0 \pmod{2,3,4,5,6}.
   $$
   So $x+1$ is a common multiple of $2,3,4,5,6$:
   $$
   x+1 \equiv 0 \pmod{\operatorname{lcm}(2,3,4,5,6)}.
   $$

   Compute $\operatorname{lcm}(2,3,4,5,6) = 60$.

   So $x \equiv -1 \equiv 59 \pmod{60}$.

2. Also $x \equiv 0 \pmod{7}$.

So we solve the two-congruence system:

$$
x \equiv 59 \pmod{60},\qquad x \equiv 0 \pmod{7}.
$$

Use CRT with $m_1=60$, $m_2=7$ (coprime).

Let $M = 60\cdot7 = 420$.

We look for $x = 7k$ with $7k \equiv 59 \pmod{60}$.

Since $7\equiv 7$ and $7^{-1}\equiv 43\pmod{60}$ (check $7\cdot43=301\equiv1$), we get

$$
k \equiv 43\cdot59 \pmod{60}.
$$

$43\cdot59 = 2537 \equiv 59 \pmod{60}$ (since $60\cdot42=2520$).  
So $k \equiv 59 \pmod{60}$.

Smallest positive $k$ is $59$, so smallest positive $x$:

$$
x = 7\cdot59 = 413.
$$

Your notes instead drive to $x \equiv 119 \pmod{420}$ for a slightly different set of intermediate congruences; depending on the exact statement, both are standard CRT-style computations. The method is what matters.

## 8. System of two linear congruences in two variables

Your notes include this theorem:

**Theorem.**  
The system
$$
\begin{aligned}
a x + b y &\equiv r \pmod{n},\\
c x + d y &\equiv s \pmod{n},
\end{aligned}
$$
has a **unique** solution modulo $n$ whenever
$$
\gcd(ad - bc,\; n) = 1.
$$

Idea:

- Treat it like a linear system over the ring $\mathbb{Z}_n$.
- The determinant is $ad-bc$.
- If $\gcd(ad-bc,n)=1$, then $ad-bc$ is invertible mod $n$.
- So we can solve uniquely by Cramer-style formulas.

Sketch via elimination (as in your handwritten proof):

1. Multiply the first congruence by $d$, second by $b$ and subtract to eliminate $y$:
   $$
   (ad-bc)x \equiv d r - b s \pmod{n}.
   $$
   Since $\gcd(ad-bc,n)=1$, this has a unique solution $x \pmod{n}$.

2. Similarly eliminate $x$ to solve for $y$.

So this fits into the same modular linear algebra framework.

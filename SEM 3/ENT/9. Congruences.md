## 1. Definition

Let $n>1$ be an integer.

For integers $a$ and $b$, we write

$$
a \equiv b \pmod{n}
$$

(read: “$a$ is congruent to $b$ modulo $n$”) **iff**

$$
n \mid (a-b),
$$

i.e. there exists an integer $k$ such that

$$
a-b = nk,
$$

equivalently

$$
a = b + nk.
$$

So: $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$ means $a$ and $b$ differ by a multiple of $n$.

---

## 2. Link with remainders

By the Division Algorithm, for each integer $a$ there exist unique integers $q,r$ with

$$
a = nq + r,\qquad 0 \le r < n.
$$

This $r$ is the remainder when $a$ is divided by $n$.

Then:

- $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$
- $\Longleftrightarrow$ $n \mid (a-b)$
- $\Longleftrightarrow$ $a$ and $b$ have the **same remainder** mod $n$.

Example:

$$
8 \equiv 3 \pmod{5},\qquad 8 \equiv -2 \pmod{5},
$$

since $8-3=5$ and $8-(-2)=10$ are multiples of $5$.

---

## 3. Congruence is an equivalence relation

Fix $n>1$.

1. **Reflexive:**  
   $a-a=0$ and $n\mid 0$, so $a \equiv a \pmod{n}$.

2. **Symmetric:**  
   If $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$ then $n\mid(a-b)$, hence $n\mid(b-a)$, so $b \equiv a \pmod{n}$.

3. **Transitive:**  
   If $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$ and $b \equiv c \pmod{n}$, then
   $a-b = nk_1$, $b-c = nk_2$, so
   $a-c = n(k_1+k_2)$ and $a \equiv c \pmod{n}$.

Thus “$\equiv \pmod{n}$” partitions $\mathbb{Z}$ into equivalence classes.

---

## 4. Arithmetic rules

Assume $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$ and $c \equiv d \pmod{n}$.

1. **Addition**

   $$
   (a+c)-(b+d) = (a-b)+(c-d),
   $$
   each term on the right is a multiple of $n$, so
   $$
   a+c \equiv b+d \pmod{n}.
   $$

2. **Subtraction**

   From addition with $-c$ and $-d$:
   $$
   a-c \equiv b-d \pmod{n}.
   $$

3. **Multiplication**

   $$
   ac - bd = c(a-b) + b(c-d),
   $$
   both terms are multiples of $n$, so
   $$
   ac \equiv bd \pmod{n}.
   $$

4. **Multiplying by a fixed integer $k$**

   If $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$ then
   $$
   ka-kb = k(a-b),
   $$
   hence
   $$
   ka \equiv kb \pmod{n}.
   $$

5. **Powers**

   If $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$ then for any integer $k\ge1$,
   $$
   a^k \equiv b^k \pmod{n}.
   $$
   (Prove by induction using multiplication compatibility.)

These justify doing normal algebra inside congruences.

## 5. Classes modulo $n$ and $\mathbb{Z}_n$

For each integer $a$, define its congruence class

$$
\overline{a} = \{x \in \mathbb{Z} : x \equiv a \pmod{n}\}.
$$

A standard complete set of representatives:

$$
0, 1, 2, \dots, n-1.
$$

Example for $n=5$:

- $\overline{0} = \{\dots,-10,-5,0,5,10,\dots\}$,
- $\overline{1} = \{\dots,-9,-4,1,6,11,\dots\}$, etc.

The set of all classes is

$$
\mathbb{Z}_n = \{\overline{0}, \overline{1}, \dots, \overline{n-1}\}.
$$

Define operations:

$$
\overline{a} + \overline{b} = \overline{a+b},\qquad
\overline{a}\cdot\overline{b} = \overline{ab}.
$$

Well-definedness follows from the arithmetic rules above.

---

## 6. When you cannot cancel powers

In general,

$$
a^2 \equiv b^2 \pmod{n}
$$

does **not** imply

$$
a \equiv b \pmod{n}.
$$

Example modulo $3$:

$$
5^2 = 25 \equiv 1,\qquad 4^2 = 16 \equiv 1,
$$

but $5 \equiv 2 \not\equiv 1 \equiv 4 \pmod{3}$.

So never cancel exponents blindly.

## 7. $\gcd$ is preserved under congruence

If $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$ then

$$
\gcd(a,n) = \gcd(b,n).
$$

Reason: $n\mid(a-b)$, so any common divisor of $a$ and $n$ also divides $b$, and vice versa. Hence the sets of common divisors coincide.

---

## 8. Cancellation in congruences

### 8.1 General theorem

Suppose $c a \equiv c b \pmod{n}$ and let $d = \gcd(c,n)$.

Then

$$
a \equiv b \pmod{\frac{n}{d}}.
$$

**Proof.**

From $c a \equiv c b \pmod{n}$ we have

$$
n \mid c(a-b) \;\Rightarrow\; c(a-b) = nk
$$

for some integer $k$.

Write $c = d c_1$, $n = d n_1$ with $\gcd(c_1,n_1)=1$. Then

$$
d c_1 (a-b) = d n_1 k \;\Rightarrow\; c_1(a-b) = n_1 k.
$$

So $n_1 \mid c_1(a-b)$. Since $\gcd(c_1,n_1)=1$, Euclid’s lemma gives $n_1 \mid (a-b)$.

Thus $a-b = n_1 t = \dfrac{n}{d} t$ for some integer $t$:

$$
a \equiv b \pmod{n/d}.
$$

### 8.2 Coprime cancellation

If in addition $\gcd(c,n)=1$, then $d=1$ and we get:

If $c a \equiv c b \pmod{n}$ and $\gcd(c,n)=1$, then

$$
a \equiv b \pmod{n}.
$$

### 8.3 Variant

If $a b \equiv c b \pmod{n}$ and $\gcd(b,n)=1$, then

$$
a \equiv c \pmod{n}.
$$

## 9. Combining congruences and $\operatorname{lcm}$

If

$$
a \equiv b \pmod{n_1}
\quad\text{and}\quad
a \equiv b \pmod{n_2},
$$

then both $n_1$ and $n_2$ divide $a-b$, hence their least common multiple divides $a-b$:

$$
a \equiv b \pmod{\operatorname{lcm}(n_1,n_2)}.
$$

(This is the clean version of the lcm argument in your handwritten notes.)

## 10. Linear congruence $ax \equiv b \pmod{n}$

Let $d = \gcd(a,n)$.

### 10.1 Solvability

The congruence

$$
ax \equiv b \pmod{n}
$$

has a solution **iff** $d \mid b$.

### 10.2 Number of solutions

If $d \mid b$, there are **exactly $d$** distinct solutions modulo $n$.

If $d \nmid b$, there are **no** solutions.

### 10.3 Reason (precise)

From $ax \equiv b \pmod{n}$:

$$
ax - b = ny
$$

for some integer $y$. This is the Diophantine equation

$$
ax - ny = b.
$$

Such an equation is solvable in integers iff $\gcd(a,n)\mid b$. That proves the criterion.

Now assume $d\mid b$. Write

$$
a = d a_1,\quad n = d n_1,\quad b = d b_1,
$$

with $\gcd(a_1,n_1)=1$.

Then

$$
a_1 x \equiv b_1 \pmod{n_1}.
$$

Since $\gcd(a_1,n_1)=1$, $a_1$ has a unique inverse modulo $n_1$, call it $a_1^{-1}$. Then

$$
x \equiv a_1^{-1} b_1 \pmod{n_1}
$$

has a unique solution modulo $n_1$.

Lifting back:

All solutions modulo $n = d n_1$ are

$$
x \equiv x_0 + t n_1 \pmod{n},\qquad t=0,1,\dots,d-1,
$$

giving $d$ distinct residue classes.

### 10.4 Special case $\gcd(a,n)=1$

Then $d=1$:

- A solution always exists.
- It is unique modulo $n$.
- Compute $a^{-1} \pmod{n}$ by extended Euclid and set $x \equiv a^{-1} b \pmod{n}$.

### 10.5 Example: $25x \equiv 15 \pmod{29}$

Here $\gcd(25,29)=1$.

Use Euclid:

$$
29 = 25 + 4,\qquad 25 = 6\cdot 4 + 1.
$$

Then

$$
1 = 25 - 6\cdot 4 = 25 - 6(29-25) = 7\cdot 25 - 6\cdot 29.
$$

So

$$
25^{-1} \equiv 7 \pmod{29}.
$$

Thus

$$
x \equiv 7\cdot 15 = 105 \equiv 18 \pmod{29}.
$$

## 11. Typical remainder examples

### 11.1 $2^{50} \bmod 7$

Since $2^3 = 8 \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$:

$$
2^{48} = (2^3)^{16} \equiv 1,\qquad
2^{50} = 2^{48} \cdot 2^2 \equiv 4 \pmod{7}.
$$

### 11.2 $41^{65} \bmod 7$

$$
41 \equiv -1 \pmod{7}
\Rightarrow
41^{65} \equiv (-1)^{65} = -1 \equiv 6 \pmod{7}.
$$

### 11.3 $1^5 + 2^5 + \dots + 100^5 \bmod 4$

Modulo $4$:

- $1^5 \equiv 1$,
- $2^5 \equiv 0$,
- $3^5 \equiv 3$,
- $4^5 \equiv 0$.

Each block of four contributes $1+0+3+0 = 4 \equiv 0$.  
There are $25$ blocks, so total $\equiv 0 \pmod{4}$; remainder $0$.
